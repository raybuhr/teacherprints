Talk Time

Talk Time is the total amount of speech for each Speaker.

During your lesson, you spoke XX% of the time and the Students spoke XX% of the time. For every second of Student Talk Time, there were about #.# seconds of Teacher Talk Time.

You predicted that Teacher Talk would make up ~##% of the lesson, and Student Talk would make up ~##%. How does this compare to what you see in the Speaker Wheel?

If the values are close, this indicates a match between your plan and your practice. You have a good sense of how much you and your students will talk during this type of lesson!

Close +

If the values are rather different, this indicates a mismatch between your plan and your practice. Consider reviewing the distribution of Teacher and Student Talk for similar lessons, to see if this pattern is typical for you.

If this pattern is typical for you, consider what changes you could make to help your actions be more in line with your expectations. It may be useful to review the lesson statement-by-statement Audiozation to see how the classroom conversation played out.

Typical +

If this pattern is not typical for you, consider what factors in or out of today’s class may have led to the talk distribution shown above. Are these things you might need to manage more consciously in the future?

Pauses typically indicate that a speaker is waiting for a response or thinking about how they want to respond. Given the type of lesson, would you expect many pauses between speakers? What about long pauses or periods of silence? For a more detailed look at how pauses were distributed throughout your lesson, see the statement-by-statement Audiozation.

Overlaps occur when the teacher and students are talking at the same time. Given the type of lesson, would you expect there to be multiple voices active at the same time?

If so, you can review the statement-by-statement Audiozation to see if there are overlaps that took place outside of small group activities, games, or discussions where people sometimes talk simultaneously.

Yes +

If not, overlaps may indicate that people are interrupting each other, rather than listening actively to others’ ideas. If there are a lot of these kinds of overlaps, this may be a useful area to focus on for improvement.

No +
